
\begin{abstract}
  Browser fuzzers commonly identify security vulnerabilities through crash
  testing and detect cross-browser and cross-version regressions through
  differential testing. This study introduces \emph{under-invalidation}, a
  browser correctness defect that occurs when a browser incorrectly updates
  its internal state following a style change. Because these defects depend on
  complex interactions among often novel web platform features, conventional
  differential testing has difficulty detecting them. To address this
  limitation, this study presents \toolname, a fuzzer that applies a
  self-differential oracle to large bug candidates and then minimizes and
  clusters detected failures to reduce developers' review burden. In the
  evaluation, \toolname tested hundreds of bug candidates per minute and
  identified \NumBugsChromeWeights bugs in Chrome and \NumBugsFfxWeights bugs
  in Firefox. Minimization reduced the review burden by approximately
  \StyleElementOriginalToMinimizedFactor{}, and clustering increased the
  overall reduction to \StyleElementOriginalToClusteredFactor{}, while both
  processes accounted for only \RuntimePercentMinClust\ of the total runtime.
  Of the detected defects, developers confirmed \NumBugsNovel as novel bugs in
  Chrome and Firefox, and \NumBugsFixed have been fixed. Mozilla Firefox
  developers have also incorporated \toolname into their internal fuzzing
  workflow. These findings demonstrate that self-differential testing can
  efficiently detect under-invalidation defects while substantially reducing
  the effort required to review results.
\end{abstract}
