
\begin{abstract}
  Fuzzers are widely used to find
    security-related bugs in web browser through crash testing,
    and to discover cross-browser and cross-version regressions
    via differential testing.
  We introduce a new type of browser correctness bug,
    \emph{under-invalidation}, which occurs when a browser incorrectly
    updates its internal state after a style update.
  Under-invalidation bugs are difficult to find
    through differential testing due to their reliance on
    complex relations across typically-novel web platform features.
  But under-invalidation bugs allow for self-differential testing,
    which tests a browser rendering engine against itself,
    which allows for their efficient discovery.

  We therefore develop \toolname, a fuzzer that
    tests a new self-differential oracle
    against large bug candidates
    to efficiently find under-invalidation bugs.
  It then minimizes and clusters the resulting bugs
    to reduce the review burden for developers.
  In our evaluation,
    \toolname is able to find dozens of bugs in both Chrome and Firefox
    (\NumBugsChromeWeights in Chrome and \NumBugsFfxWeights in Firefox)
    while testing hundreds of bug candidates per minute.
  \toolname's minimization and clustering passes
    then reduce the review load by approximately
    \StyleElementOriginalToMinimizedFactor{} through minimization
    and \StyleElementOriginalToClusteredFactor{} overall after clustering,
    all while requiring only \RuntimePercentMinClust\ of the runtime.
  Finally, we show that \toolname finds
    \NumBugsNovel novel bugs,
    reported to and accepted by the developers,
    in Chrome and Firefox.
  Of these, \NumBugsFixed bugs have already been fixed,
    and the developers of Mozilla Firefox
    have adopted \toolname in their internal fuzzing workflow.
\end{abstract}
